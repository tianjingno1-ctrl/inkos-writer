# 第3章 The First Move

Three days passed. Ella worked under Ethan’s wing, learning the business. He showed her how to read contracts and manage his calendar, correcting her phrasing with the patience of a man who had nothing to prove and everything to hide.

But she felt him watching. Testing.

On the fourth day, she stayed late. The building emptied in stages—executives first, then assistants, then security making rounds with keys that chimed against their belts. By the time Ella reached the parking lot, it was empty except for her car and the sodium lights humming overhead.

Three men stepped out from behind a truck. Pureblood enforcers. She recognized the emblem on their jackets: the Vance family crest.

“Ella Vance,” the leader said, rolling his shoulders. “We have orders to collect a sample. Routine check. If you’re clean, we’ll let you go.”

She backed up. Her heart slammed against her ribs. A sample meant they’d draw blood—and if they sensed the hybrid taint, they’d drag her to the Council.

Her arm burned. The silver mark was hidden under her sleeve, but she felt it pulsing, as if it knew its own name and hated being spoken aloud.

“I’m an employee of Redwood. You have no jurisdiction here.”

“We have every jurisdiction. The Council supersedes corporate policy.”

The leader stepped forward, reaching for her arm. His fingers smelled of iron and old violence.

Then a shot cracked the air. A bullet hit the ground at his feet, throwing up sparks.

Ethan appeared from the shadows, a smoking gun in his hand. “She’s under my protection.”

The enforcer froze. “Mr. Blackwood. This doesn’t concern you.”

“It does now.” Ethan’s voice was ice. “This is Redwood ground. The Council doesn’t draw blood on my land without a warrant. Back off.”

The enforcer snarled but backed away. “This isn’t over.”

They left. Their truck swallowed the night. Ella’s breath came back in ragged pieces.

Ella sagged against the wall. Ethan grabbed her arm and pulled her toward his car. “We need to talk.”

He drove to a diner outside the city, neon buzzing over cracked vinyl booths. In a booth, he slid a folder across the table. Inside: her birth certificate, a photo of her mother, and a page from an old prophecy written in a hand that looked too old to be human.

“Your mother was the Seer of the Lycan royal house,” he said. “Your father was King Aldric. My family was killed by the same people who killed him—the Elder Council. They want me dead because I’m the last of the Northern Lycan line. And they want you because your blood can power the blood curse that enslaves hybrids.”

She stared at him. “Why are you telling me this now?”

“Because I wanted to see if you could handle it. The other day, you saw the cable. You saw the fire. You have the gift. But you don’t control it yet.”

“And you? What do you want from me?”

“I want to stop the Council. But I can’t do it alone.” He leaned forward. Coffee steam rose between them. “I can train you. Teach you to control your visions. In return, you help me take them down.”

“And if I refuse?”

“They’ll find you. And they’ll use you to kill every hybrid alive. Including yourself.”

Ella wrapped her hands around her coffee cup. Her reflection wavered in the dark liquid, fractured by the diner lights.

She looked at the photograph again—the woman with silver-streaked hair, eyes soft in a way Ella had only ever seen in dreams. Her throat tightened. Ten years of absence sat in that smile.

“I’m in,” she said. “But I don’t trust you.”

“That’s fine.” Ethan didn’t reach for the folder. He let her keep looking. “Trust will come later.”

They sat with the coffee going cold. Outside, the highway hissed. For a few minutes, neither of them moved to fill the silence. Ella counted trucks passing in the dark, as if numbers could keep the world from changing shape again.

When she finally stood, her legs felt borrowed. Ethan held the car door without touching her. It was the closest thing to kindness she had received all week—and that, more than the prophecy, frightened her.
