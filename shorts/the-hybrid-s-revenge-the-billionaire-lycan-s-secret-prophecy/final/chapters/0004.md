# 第4章 Escape

The next night, two assassins broke into Ella’s apartment.

She woke to the sound of glass breaking. A shadow lunged for her, knife gleaming. Her vision flashed—she saw the arc of the blade a second before it swung. She rolled off the bed, grabbing a lamp to block. Ceramic cracked. Shards skittered across the floor.

The second assassin came from behind. She ducked and ran for the door. Her bare feet slipped on something wet—blood, she realized later, though whose she never learned.

Ethan crashed through the window, already shifted into a half-Lycan form. Claws tore through one assassin’s chest. He grabbed her hand—his palm too hot, too rough—and pulled her down the fire escape.

They reached his car and sped away. City lights streaked past. Ella’s teeth chattered though the night was warm.

“They found you too fast,” he said. “Someone in my company is feeding them information.”

He drove to a safe house in the woods. A two-story cabin, warded with silver. The air smelled of pine and cold metal. Every step across the threshold made the mark on her arm throb, as if approval and warning were the same thing.

Ella collapsed on the couch. Her hands wouldn’t stop shaking.

Ethan brought her a glass of water. “You used your vision.”

“I saw the knife. A second before.”

“Good. That’s the first stage. But you can’t rely on instinct. You need to train.”

She nodded, but her mind was elsewhere. “I keep hearing a woman’s voice. In my head.”

Ethan’s expression shifted. “Describe her.”

“She says she’s my mother. She called my name. She told me they’re keeping her underground.”

Ethan sat down heavily. “That’s your mother. She’s alive—in the palace dungeons. The Council keeps her there to drain her seer power.”

Ella’s throat closed. The room tilted. Alive. The word sat in her chest too large to breathe around.

“Why didn’t you tell me?”

“Because you weren’t ready to hear it and still think clearly.”

She pressed her palms to her eyes. The voice in the dark—*Daughter. Stay alive.*—was real.

When she lowered her hands, her face was wet. She didn’t wipe it.

“How long?” she whispered.

“Ten years. Maybe longer.”

Ella stared at the white rose on the pillow across the room. Fresh. Impossible. Her mother had reached her anyway, across stone and chains and a decade of silence.

She stood slowly, legs unsteady. “I’m ready now. We need to go get her.”

“No. Not yet. The Blood Moon is in ten days. That’s when the Council will try to activate the curse using her as the sacrifice. If we move before that, they’ll kill her.”

Ella’s hands balled into fists. “Ten days. That’s how long I have to get strong enough.”

“Yes. And to learn to trust me.”

She met his eyes. “That depends on what you’re not telling me.”

He held her gaze. “Fair.”

Outside, wind moved through the trees like something searching. Ella listened for footsteps on the porch and heard none. The cabin felt too quiet after the city, too honest about how far she was from anyone who could help her.

That night, she lay in the guest room, alone. A white rose appeared on the pillow, petals uncrushed, as if no hand had ever been chained long enough to forget how to send love.

She pressed it to her chest and cried. Grief and rage mixed until she could not tell them apart. Somewhere beneath both, a third feeling waited—hope, thin and fierce, like silver wire.

Ethan left a blanket on the back of the couch without a word. She heard him check the locks twice before midnight. She did not thank him. She did not need to.
