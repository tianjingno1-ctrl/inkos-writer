# 第5章 The Limit of Vision

Training began at dawn.

Ethan led her into the clearing behind the cabin. Mist clung to the grass. “The sight works on threat detection. You see danger a second or two before it happens. But you can’t control what you see. You can only react. We’re going to change that.”

He attacked her without warning. She dodged the first punch, thanks to a flash. The second caught her shoulder. She stumbled.

“Again.”

Hour after hour. By noon, she was bruised and bleeding. But she could see two seconds ahead—sometimes three. Her body learned the rhythm of pain arriving before pain landed.

He stopped and handed her a towel. “Good progress. But there’s a cost. Every use drains your energy. If you push too hard, you’ll pass out.”

She wiped her face. Sweat stung the split on her lip. “How do I last longer?”

“You need to bond with a Lycan partner. The bond shares your energy load. But that’s a permanent decision.”

She looked at him. Morning light caught the scar on his jaw she had never asked about. “You mean mark each other.”

“Yes. It’s not something to do lightly.”

She said nothing.

That afternoon, she practiced alone. She tried to trigger visions on command. She focused on the tree in front of her. She willed herself to see it fall. Nothing.

Then she thought of her mother. The vision came: a stone cell, a woman with silver hair, chains biting wrists raw. She heard a whisper: *Come to the Blood Moon. Bring the king’s blood.*

Ella collapsed, head pounding. Nausea rolled through her in waves.

Ethan found her. “You forced it.”

“I saw her. She told me to come.”

“The Blood Moon is nine days away. You’re not ready.”

“I have to try.”

“You won’t survive. Not alone.”

She looked at him. “Then teach me the bond.”

He held her eyes for a long moment. “You don’t trust me.”

“No. But I’ll risk it if it saves her.”

He stepped closer. “The bond isn’t a transaction. If we do it, we do it fully. Or it won’t work.”

She swallowed. “What does that mean?”

“It means we have to mean it. Both of us. If either of us hesitates, the mark will burn out and we’ll both go mad.”

She thought about the past week. The way he’d saved her. The way he looked at her when she was not performing strength. The way her heart beat when he was near, faster than fear alone could explain.

“I don’t know if I can mean it yet.”

Ethan held her gaze. He didn’t argue. “Then we wait.”

He turned toward the tree line, then stopped. His shoulders dropped a fraction.

“I’m not going anywhere,” he said, without looking back. “When you’re ready—if you’re ready—I’ll be here.”

That night, she stared at the ceiling. The silver mark on her arm pulsed in time with her heartbeat. She wanted to save her mother. She wanted to live. She wanted Ethan to look at her the way he’d looked at her when she’d said no—not disappointed, not patient, but present.

Through the thin wall, she heard him moving in the other room. Not leaving. Not closing a door on her.

Maybe she already meant it.

Maybe wanting was the first honest thing she had felt since the elevator cable fell.

In the dark, she pressed her palm to the silver scar and felt, faintly, the echo of Ethan’s heartbeat answering hers. Not complete. Not safe. But there.

When she finally slept, she dreamed of the cabin clearing in daylight—not training, not blood, but his hand steady when hers shook. She woke with the mark warm and knew, with a clarity that frightened her, that waiting was no longer the same as refusing.
