# 第1章 Traces of Prophecy

Ella Vance kept her head down as the three pureblood women walked past her desk.

“Shift your chair, half-breed. You’re blocking the light.” Cassie’s coffee cup tipped deliberately. Warm liquid splashed across Ella’s keyboard and dripped onto her skirt. The other two laughed.

Ella counted to five. She’d learned to count. It kept her hands from shaking.

She blotted the spill with napkins, said nothing. Behind her, someone whispered “pathetic.” She kept her face blank and kept working. The coffee smelled bitter. It soaked through her stockings and clung cold against her skin. She had learned that reaction was what they wanted—a flinch, a tear, anything that proved she didn’t belong on the forty-second floor.

At five thirty, she packed her bag and walked to the elevator. Her skirt was still damp. The workday had been long—another meeting where Lydia Vance, her so-called cousin and head of accounting, had suggested she was “better suited for a role without client contact.” Lydia had smiled when she said it, the way people smiled when they were being polite and cruel at the same time.

Ella had smiled back. She always smiled.

The elevator doors opened. She stepped inside. A man in a dark suit followed. Ethan Blackwood, CEO of Redwood Holdings. He was taller up close, with eyes the color of warm earth and a stillness that made the car feel smaller. He pressed a button and stood beside her. She caught the faint scent of cedar and rain on his coat.

The car began to rise.

Then her vision ripped open.

Steel screaming. Sparks bursting. A cable whipping like a snake. Heat on her face. The taste of copper in her mouth though nothing had bled yet. She saw the floor number change—3, 4—and the ceiling crack. In her sight, the cable would come down right where he stood.

Her body moved before her mind caught up.

She lunged. Her fingers closed around his sleeve and dragged him sideways. A second later, the cable punched through the floor panel, screeching as it coiled where he’d been. Dust rained down. The car lurched. Ella’s knees hit the wall hard enough to bruise.

Silence. Then his voice, low and calm. “You just saved my life.”

Ella’s legs shook. “I don’t know why I did that. I saw—I thought I saw something.”

He looked at her. His gaze was steady, measuring, as if he were reading a document only he could see. “It happens sometimes. A flash of danger. Some people call it instinct.”

He didn’t mention her eyes. He didn’t mention silver. She felt a flicker of relief—and something else, a prickle at the base of her skull, like a door opening one inch and closing again.

The elevator reached the top floor. He stepped out, then paused. “Good night, Miss Vance.”

She stayed inside until the doors closed. Her reflection in the polished steel looked ordinary. Brown eyes. Damp skirt. Hands that would not stop trembling.

When she finally got home, rain had started. She locked the door and collapsed on her bed. Her right arm burned. She pushed up her sleeve and stared.

A silver mark coiled around her forearm, delicate as frost. She touched it. Pain flared, sharp and clean, as if someone had drawn a hot wire beneath her skin.

In the bathroom mirror, her eyes were normal. Brown. But for an instant—one heartbeat—they turned silver, bright enough to throw light against the tile.

She knocked a glass off the counter. It shattered. When she looked again, the silver was gone.

Her phone buzzed. Unknown number: *You are not what they think. Be careful.*

She dropped the phone as if burned.

That night, she didn’t sleep. She kept the lights on and watched the shadows. Every creak in the building sounded like footsteps. Every gust against the window sounded like someone trying the lock.

Somewhere between midnight and dawn, she dreamed of a woman in chains who whispered her name.
