# 第10章 Father's Vault

The horns grew louder with every step.

Ella descended with a torch of silver fire in her palm. Ethan followed, blade drawn. The stair opened into a circular vault carved from black stone. At its center, a pedestal of obsidian held a sphere of liquid light.

Ancient words ringed the wall: *For the heir who completes the prophecy, the blood of the ancestor shall reveal the peace eternal.*

“Keep moving,” Ethan said. Dust shook loose from the ceiling. “Whatever’s up there isn’t waiting.”

Ella touched the sphere.

Images flooded her—the First King, the founding packs, the moon bargain, the feud between her father’s house and Ethan’s. She saw wolves kneeling not from fear but from law written in blood. Then the prophecy in silver script: *When she marks with the enemy line, the blood feud ends.*

She turned, breathless. “Our families were enemies for centuries. The bond wasn’t luck. It was the seal.”

Ethan’s face had gone pale. “Then the mark was always meant to be you.”

The sphere cracked. A glass-clear dagger floated into her grip—the Unity Blade, forged to bind packs by consent, not chains. It weighed almost nothing. It hummed against her palm like a living thing agreeing to be held.

The vault shuddered. Not magic—impact. A ram hitting the outer gate.

“We go,” Ethan said.

They climbed two hundred steps at a run and burst into the throne room. Jason met them, cheek split open, blood on his teeth.

“Five hundred loyalists at the wall,” he said. “Marcus’s old guard. They want the palace back before you can crown yourself queen of anything.”

Ella sheathed the Unity Blade at her hip. “Where’s Bjorn?”

“Holding the east wall with half his line. The other half broke when they saw the ram.”

Ella didn’t hesitate. She went out front.

The loyalist commander screamed from horseback. “She’s a half-breed witch! Take the palace back for pure blood!”

His wolves charged.

Ella stood in the gateway, silver fire crawling up her arms. “I am Aldric’s daughter. I carry the Unity Blade. Turn back, or answer to the moon.”

Some hesitated. Most did not.

The fight was ugly and short. Ella’s sight turned the lane into a trap—she called angles to Bjorn’s line as he rallied, cut down the ram crew with the blade’s flat, paralyzing without killing. Ethan tore through the center in half-shift, a blur of black fur and controlled rage. When the commander fell, the rest broke.

Silence settled over blood and stone.

Ella raised the blade so every survivor could see it. Torchlight ran along the glass edge like a second moon.

“Tomorrow at noon,” she said, voice carrying, “pureblood privilege dies in this hall. Hybrid and pure wolf stand equal under one law. Spread the word tonight.”

No one cheered. They were too tired. But they listened. Ella watched faces in the crowd—fear, calculation, the first fragile shape of belief.

Jason slumped against the wall, laughing once in disbelief. “That’s two wars in one day.”

Ella looked at the blade, then at Ethan’s bloodied knuckles, then at the sky where smoke still climbed.

“One day,” she said. “Two wins. We’re not done yet.”

She did not feel like a queen. She felt like a woman who had run out of stairs and found, at the bottom, the weapon her father had left and the man her mother’s prophecy had demanded. That would have to be enough until dawn.

Ethan wiped blood from his knuckles and stood beside her without touching the blade. “Tomorrow,” he said, “they sign or they leave.”

“Tomorrow,” she agreed, “we make them choose.”

Behind them, Bjorn’s wolves dragged the ram aside like discarded timber. The palace still smelled of smoke, but for the first time since Marcus fell, the wind off the wall carried no battle horn—only rain coming in from the north.
