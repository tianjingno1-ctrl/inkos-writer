# The Hybrid’s Revenge: The Billionaire Lycan’s Secret Prophecy

## 开篇钩子

Ella’s mind went white. She saw the cable snap before it fell—a blur of steel, tearing through the elevator shaft. Her hand shot out without thinking, grabbing the man beside her and yanking him back. The broken cable slammed into the floor where he’d been standing.
Ethan Blackwood turned. His gaze swept over her. “You moved fast.”
She let go of his sleeve, heart hammering. “I just—I saw it.”
He didn’t say anything else. But as he stepped out on the top floor, he clenched his fist. Blood beaded between his fingers—a wound that hadn’t been there before.
Ella walked home in the rain, rubbing her arm where a new burn seared under her sleeve. She didn’t dare look.
That night, she dreamed of a woman in chains who whispered her name.

## 第1章 Traces of Prophecy

Ella Vance kept her head down as the three pureblood women walked past her desk.

“Shift your chair, half-breed. You’re blocking the light.” Cassie’s coffee cup tipped deliberately. Warm liquid splashed across Ella’s keyboard and dripped onto her skirt. The other two laughed.

Ella counted to five. She’d learned to count. It kept her hands from shaking.

She blotted the spill with napkins, said nothing. Behind her, someone whispered “pathetic.” She kept her face blank and kept working. The coffee smelled bitter. It soaked through her stockings and clung cold against her skin. She had learned that reaction was what they wanted—a flinch, a tear, anything that proved she didn’t belong on the forty-second floor.

At five thirty, she packed her bag and walked to the elevator. Her skirt was still damp. The workday had been long—another meeting where Lydia Vance, her so-called cousin and head of accounting, had suggested she was “better suited for a role without client contact.” Lydia had smiled when she said it, the way people smiled when they were being polite and cruel at the same time.

Ella had smiled back. She always smiled.

The elevator doors opened. She stepped inside. A man in a dark suit followed. Ethan Blackwood, CEO of Redwood Holdings. He was taller up close, with eyes the color of warm earth and a stillness that made the car feel smaller. He pressed a button and stood beside her. She caught the faint scent of cedar and rain on his coat.

The car began to rise.

Then her vision ripped open.

Steel screaming. Sparks bursting. A cable whipping like a snake. Heat on her face. The taste of copper in her mouth though nothing had bled yet. She saw the floor number change—3, 4—and the ceiling crack. In her sight, the cable would come down right where he stood.

Her body moved before her mind caught up.

She lunged. Her fingers closed around his sleeve and dragged him sideways. A second later, the cable punched through the floor panel, screeching as it coiled where he’d been. Dust rained down. The car lurched. Ella’s knees hit the wall hard enough to bruise.

Silence. Then his voice, low and calm. “You just saved my life.”

Ella’s legs shook. “I don’t know why I did that. I saw—I thought I saw something.”

He looked at her. His gaze was steady, measuring, as if he were reading a document only he could see. “It happens sometimes. A flash of danger. Some people call it instinct.”

He didn’t mention her eyes. He didn’t mention silver. She felt a flicker of relief—and something else, a prickle at the base of her skull, like a door opening one inch and closing again.

The elevator reached the top floor. He stepped out, then paused. “Good night, Miss Vance.”

She stayed inside until the doors closed. Her reflection in the polished steel looked ordinary. Brown eyes. Damp skirt. Hands that would not stop trembling.

When she finally got home, rain had started. She locked the door and collapsed on her bed. Her right arm burned. She pushed up her sleeve and stared.

A silver mark coiled around her forearm, delicate as frost. She touched it. Pain flared, sharp and clean, as if someone had drawn a hot wire beneath her skin.

In the bathroom mirror, her eyes were normal. Brown. But for an instant—one heartbeat—they turned silver, bright enough to throw light against the tile.

She knocked a glass off the counter. It shattered. When she looked again, the silver was gone.

Her phone buzzed. Unknown number: *You are not what they think. Be careful.*

She dropped the phone as if burned.

That night, she didn’t sleep. She kept the lights on and watched the shadows. Every creak in the building sounded like footsteps. Every gust against the window sounded like someone trying the lock.

Somewhere between midnight and dawn, she dreamed of a woman in chains who whispered her name.

## 第2章 The Promotion Trap

Ella arrived at work the next morning to find the office buzzing.

Whispers followed her from the lobby to her desk. Someone had screenshot the six-a.m. email and posted it in the department chat before she even logged in. *Private assistant to the CEO.* The title sat on her screen like a target painted on her back.

Cassie blocked her path, sneering. “Looks like someone special got an upgrade. Private assistant to the CEO. Congratulations, half-breed.”

Ella’s stomach dropped. The email had come at six a.m. She hadn’t applied for it.

Lydia appeared behind Cassie, clicking across the marble floor. Her eyes were ice. “This is a mistake. She’s a temp with no qualifications.”

Ethan Blackwood’s voice cut through from the elevator. “The decision is made. Ella, with me.”

She grabbed her bag and walked toward him. The whole floor watched. Cassie’s smile did not reach her eyes. Lydia’s fingers drummed on her tablet, knuckles white.

In the private elevator, she said, “I didn’t ask for this.”

“You didn’t need to.” He pressed a button. The car rose without stopping. “You have potential. I want to see where it goes.”

She frowned. Potential? He didn’t explain. He only looked at the numbers lighting above the door, jaw set, as if the conversation were already finished.

The top floor office was wide and glass-walled. City spread below like a map she was not allowed to touch. She noticed a file open on his desk—a photograph of a woman with silver-streaked hair. Her mother.

Ella’s chest tightened. “Who is that?”

Ethan closed the folder. “Someone from a past life. Don’t worry about it.”

The dismissal landed like a door shut in her face. She wanted to argue. She swallowed the words and nodded instead.

He assigned her a desk in the outer office. She sorted files, answered calls, learned the rhythm of his day—meetings at nine, silence at noon, a call with overseas investors that never seemed to end. At midday, she went down to the archive room to pull old contracts.

The basement smelled of dust and old paper. Fluorescent lights buzzed. As she reached for a box, the vision came again. Flames. Smoke. A fire alarm. She saw the sprinklers fail, the stairwell fill with heat, two men too close to a sparking panel to hear reason.

She ran out of the archive room and found two janitors near a faulty electrical panel, sparks flying. She grabbed both by their collars and hauled them back. “Move. Now.”

Moments later, the panel caught fire. The alarm shrieked. Smoke rolled along the ceiling in oily waves.

Ethan arrived as the sprinklers finally kicked on. Water soaked his shirt. He looked at her, rainwater-dark hair plastered to his forehead. “You have a talent for being in the right place.”

She said nothing. But she caught Lydia watching from the hallway, suspicion in her eyes—and something hungrier beneath it.

That afternoon, Ella found a manila envelope on her desk. No return address. Inside: a copy of her birth certificate, stamped with a mark she didn’t recognize. A handwritten note: *We know what you are. Leave before we take action.*

She shoved it into her bag. Her pulse hammered in her throat.

On her way out, she passed Lydia’s office. The door was open. Lydia was on the phone, voice low: “...she triggered something today. I’m sending my report tonight. Yes, the Council needs to know.”

Ella walked faster. The elevator felt too slow. The street felt too exposed.

At home, she locked the door and slid to the floor. The silver mark on her arm was pulsing. She hadn’t even told Ethan about the note. She wasn’t sure she could trust anyone with it.

In the dark, she heard a whisper. *Daughter. Stay alive. They are coming.*

She jerked her head up. No one was there.

But on her bed lay a single white rose, fresh, as if just picked. Petals cool against her palm. No note. No explanation.

She didn’t sleep. She kept the rose in her hand until her fingers cramped, afraid that if she let go, it would vanish like everything else her mother had ever tried to send.

## 第3章 The First Move

Three days passed. Ella worked under Ethan’s wing, learning the business. He showed her how to read contracts and manage his calendar, correcting her phrasing with the patience of a man who had nothing to prove and everything to hide.

But she felt him watching. Testing.

On the fourth day, she stayed late. The building emptied in stages—executives first, then assistants, then security making rounds with keys that chimed against their belts. By the time Ella reached the parking lot, it was empty except for her car and the sodium lights humming overhead.

Three men stepped out from behind a truck. Pureblood enforcers. She recognized the emblem on their jackets: the Vance family crest.

“Ella Vance,” the leader said, rolling his shoulders. “We have orders to collect a sample. Routine check. If you’re clean, we’ll let you go.”

She backed up. Her heart slammed against her ribs. A sample meant they’d draw blood—and if they sensed the hybrid taint, they’d drag her to the Council.

Her arm burned. The silver mark was hidden under her sleeve, but she felt it pulsing, as if it knew its own name and hated being spoken aloud.

“I’m an employee of Redwood. You have no jurisdiction here.”

“We have every jurisdiction. The Council supersedes corporate policy.”

The leader stepped forward, reaching for her arm. His fingers smelled of iron and old violence.

Then a shot cracked the air. A bullet hit the ground at his feet, throwing up sparks.

Ethan appeared from the shadows, a smoking gun in his hand. “She’s under my protection.”

The enforcer froze. “Mr. Blackwood. This doesn’t concern you.”

“It does now.” Ethan’s voice was ice. “This is Redwood ground. The Council doesn’t draw blood on my land without a warrant. Back off.”

The enforcer snarled but backed away. “This isn’t over.”

They left. Their truck swallowed the night. Ella’s breath came back in ragged pieces.

Ella sagged against the wall. Ethan grabbed her arm and pulled her toward his car. “We need to talk.”

He drove to a diner outside the city, neon buzzing over cracked vinyl booths. In a booth, he slid a folder across the table. Inside: her birth certificate, a photo of her mother, and a page from an old prophecy written in a hand that looked too old to be human.

“Your mother was the Seer of the Lycan royal house,” he said. “Your father was King Aldric. My family was killed by the same people who killed him—the Elder Council. They want me dead because I’m the last of the Northern Lycan line. And they want you because your blood can power the blood curse that enslaves hybrids.”

She stared at him. “Why are you telling me this now?”

“Because I wanted to see if you could handle it. The other day, you saw the cable. You saw the fire. You have the gift. But you don’t control it yet.”

“And you? What do you want from me?”

“I want to stop the Council. But I can’t do it alone.” He leaned forward. Coffee steam rose between them. “I can train you. Teach you to control your visions. In return, you help me take them down.”

“And if I refuse?”

“They’ll find you. And they’ll use you to kill every hybrid alive. Including yourself.”

Ella wrapped her hands around her coffee cup. Her reflection wavered in the dark liquid, fractured by the diner lights.

She looked at the photograph again—the woman with silver-streaked hair, eyes soft in a way Ella had only ever seen in dreams. Her throat tightened. Ten years of absence sat in that smile.

“I’m in,” she said. “But I don’t trust you.”

“That’s fine.” Ethan didn’t reach for the folder. He let her keep looking. “Trust will come later.”

They sat with the coffee going cold. Outside, the highway hissed. For a few minutes, neither of them moved to fill the silence. Ella counted trucks passing in the dark, as if numbers could keep the world from changing shape again.

When she finally stood, her legs felt borrowed. Ethan held the car door without touching her. It was the closest thing to kindness she had received all week—and that, more than the prophecy, frightened her.

## 第4章 Escape

The next night, two assassins broke into Ella’s apartment.

She woke to the sound of glass breaking. A shadow lunged for her, knife gleaming. Her vision flashed—she saw the arc of the blade a second before it swung. She rolled off the bed, grabbing a lamp to block. Ceramic cracked. Shards skittered across the floor.

The second assassin came from behind. She ducked and ran for the door. Her bare feet slipped on something wet—blood, she realized later, though whose she never learned.

Ethan crashed through the window, already shifted into a half-Lycan form. Claws tore through one assassin’s chest. He grabbed her hand—his palm too hot, too rough—and pulled her down the fire escape.

They reached his car and sped away. City lights streaked past. Ella’s teeth chattered though the night was warm.

“They found you too fast,” he said. “Someone in my company is feeding them information.”

He drove to a safe house in the woods. A two-story cabin, warded with silver. The air smelled of pine and cold metal. Every step across the threshold made the mark on her arm throb, as if approval and warning were the same thing.

Ella collapsed on the couch. Her hands wouldn’t stop shaking.

Ethan brought her a glass of water. “You used your vision.”

“I saw the knife. A second before.”

“Good. That’s the first stage. But you can’t rely on instinct. You need to train.”

She nodded, but her mind was elsewhere. “I keep hearing a woman’s voice. In my head.”

Ethan’s expression shifted. “Describe her.”

“She says she’s my mother. She called my name. She told me they’re keeping her underground.”

Ethan sat down heavily. “That’s your mother. She’s alive—in the palace dungeons. The Council keeps her there to drain her seer power.”

Ella’s throat closed. The room tilted. Alive. The word sat in her chest too large to breathe around.

“Why didn’t you tell me?”

“Because you weren’t ready to hear it and still think clearly.”

She pressed her palms to her eyes. The voice in the dark—*Daughter. Stay alive.*—was real.

When she lowered her hands, her face was wet. She didn’t wipe it.

“How long?” she whispered.

“Ten years. Maybe longer.”

Ella stared at the white rose on the pillow across the room. Fresh. Impossible. Her mother had reached her anyway, across stone and chains and a decade of silence.

She stood slowly, legs unsteady. “I’m ready now. We need to go get her.”

“No. Not yet. The Blood Moon is in ten days. That’s when the Council will try to activate the curse using her as the sacrifice. If we move before that, they’ll kill her.”

Ella’s hands balled into fists. “Ten days. That’s how long I have to get strong enough.”

“Yes. And to learn to trust me.”

She met his eyes. “That depends on what you’re not telling me.”

He held her gaze. “Fair.”

Outside, wind moved through the trees like something searching. Ella listened for footsteps on the porch and heard none. The cabin felt too quiet after the city, too honest about how far she was from anyone who could help her.

That night, she lay in the guest room, alone. A white rose appeared on the pillow, petals uncrushed, as if no hand had ever been chained long enough to forget how to send love.

She pressed it to her chest and cried. Grief and rage mixed until she could not tell them apart. Somewhere beneath both, a third feeling waited—hope, thin and fierce, like silver wire.

Ethan left a blanket on the back of the couch without a word. She heard him check the locks twice before midnight. She did not thank him. She did not need to.

## 第5章 The Limit of Vision

Training began at dawn.

Ethan led her into the clearing behind the cabin. Mist clung to the grass. “The sight works on threat detection. You see danger a second or two before it happens. But you can’t control what you see. You can only react. We’re going to change that.”

He attacked her without warning. She dodged the first punch, thanks to a flash. The second caught her shoulder. She stumbled.

“Again.”

Hour after hour. By noon, she was bruised and bleeding. But she could see two seconds ahead—sometimes three. Her body learned the rhythm of pain arriving before pain landed.

He stopped and handed her a towel. “Good progress. But there’s a cost. Every use drains your energy. If you push too hard, you’ll pass out.”

She wiped her face. Sweat stung the split on her lip. “How do I last longer?”

“You need to bond with a Lycan partner. The bond shares your energy load. But that’s a permanent decision.”

She looked at him. Morning light caught the scar on his jaw she had never asked about. “You mean mark each other.”

“Yes. It’s not something to do lightly.”

She said nothing.

That afternoon, she practiced alone. She tried to trigger visions on command. She focused on the tree in front of her. She willed herself to see it fall. Nothing.

Then she thought of her mother. The vision came: a stone cell, a woman with silver hair, chains biting wrists raw. She heard a whisper: *Come to the Blood Moon. Bring the king’s blood.*

Ella collapsed, head pounding. Nausea rolled through her in waves.

Ethan found her. “You forced it.”

“I saw her. She told me to come.”

“The Blood Moon is nine days away. You’re not ready.”

“I have to try.”

“You won’t survive. Not alone.”

She looked at him. “Then teach me the bond.”

He held her eyes for a long moment. “You don’t trust me.”

“No. But I’ll risk it if it saves her.”

He stepped closer. “The bond isn’t a transaction. If we do it, we do it fully. Or it won’t work.”

She swallowed. “What does that mean?”

“It means we have to mean it. Both of us. If either of us hesitates, the mark will burn out and we’ll both go mad.”

She thought about the past week. The way he’d saved her. The way he looked at her when she was not performing strength. The way her heart beat when he was near, faster than fear alone could explain.

“I don’t know if I can mean it yet.”

Ethan held her gaze. He didn’t argue. “Then we wait.”

He turned toward the tree line, then stopped. His shoulders dropped a fraction.

“I’m not going anywhere,” he said, without looking back. “When you’re ready—if you’re ready—I’ll be here.”

That night, she stared at the ceiling. The silver mark on her arm pulsed in time with her heartbeat. She wanted to save her mother. She wanted to live. She wanted Ethan to look at her the way he’d looked at her when she’d said no—not disappointed, not patient, but present.

Through the thin wall, she heard him moving in the other room. Not leaving. Not closing a door on her.

Maybe she already meant it.

Maybe wanting was the first honest thing she had felt since the elevator cable fell.

In the dark, she pressed her palm to the silver scar and felt, faintly, the echo of Ethan’s heartbeat answering hers. Not complete. Not safe. But there.

When she finally slept, she dreamed of the cabin clearing in daylight—not training, not blood, but his hand steady when hers shook. She woke with the mark warm and knew, with a clarity that frightened her, that waiting was no longer the same as refusing.

## 第6章 The Diary

On the fifth day, Ethan gave her a worn leather book. “Your mother’s diary. I retrieved it years ago.”

Ella took it with trembling hands. The cover smelled of old paper and lavender, a scent so domestic it hurt. She opened it.

*To my child, if you ever read this—know that Marcus Vance is not your uncle by blood alone. He killed your father. He chained me in the depths to feed the blood curse. The only way to break it is to merge the two bloodlines: my foresight and your father’s Lycan kingship. That can only happen through a true love bond. Not a contract. Truth.*

Ella looked up. “She said the bond must be true.”

Ethan was leaning against the wall. “I know.”

“Then you and I... we can’t fake it.”

“No. And that’s why I didn’t push you.”

She closed the diary. Her thumb traced the worn edge where her mother’s hand must have rested a thousand times. “I need to know why you’re really doing this. You said your family was killed. But you never told me who.”

Ethan’s face went hard. “The Elder Council. Led by Marcus Vance. They burned our estate when I was twelve. My parents, my sister—I was the only survivor. I swore I’d burn them all.”

“So this is revenge for you.”

“Yes. For you, it’s family.”

She held his gaze. “Then we have the same goal.”

“But not the same reason.”

“Does it matter?”

He took a step closer. “Yes. Because the bond needs more than shared enemy. It needs shared trust.”

They stood in silence. Fire crackled in the hearth. Rain tapped the window in steady beats.

Then Ella said, “I want to try.”

“The bond?”

“Yes. I want to trust you. I want to be strong enough to save her. Show me what to do.”

He took her hand. His calluses scraped her palm. “It starts with a cut. Our blood against each other.”

They sat on the floor. He drew a knife and cut her palm, then his own. He pressed their hands together.

The world shuddered.

She saw fragments: a boy hiding in a closet while flames consumed his home. A man in his twenties swearing over graves. A shadow of her own face reflected in his eyes.

She felt his loss. And she felt his hope—in her.

When they broke apart, both were breathing hard.

Neither spoke. The cabin was very quiet. Ella could still feel the boy in the closet—the heat, the smoke, the door that wouldn’t open. And beneath it, Ethan’s hope, raw and unguarded, pressed against her like a second heartbeat.

“You lost them,” she said finally.

“Yes.”

“And you still—” She stopped.

Ethan turned his palm up. A thin silver scar glowed across it. “It started. It’s not complete yet. But it started.”

She looked at her own hand. A matching scar. Warm. Something that had already begun without her permission.

“I felt you,” she said.

“I felt you too.”

They sat on the floor a while longer, hands apart but close, as if letting go too fast might break whatever had just opened between them.

From somewhere deep, she heard her mother’s voice: *Good. Now come.*

The Blood Moon was five days away.

Ella closed the diary and held it to her chest. For the first time since the elevator, she did not feel alone in the fight. She felt paired—imperfect, unfinished, but real.

Outside, the Blood Moon hung low and red through the trees. Three days. She could count them on one hand and still not have enough fingers for everything at stake.

## 第7章 Blood Moon Infiltration

Five days later, they moved at dusk.
Ethan had forged documents to enter the palace under the guise of Council suppliers. Ella wore a hooded cloak to hide her face.
The palace was vast, carved from black stone. Torches lined the walls. They passed through the outer halls without incident—the guards were focused on the Blood Moon preparations in the central courtyard.
But as they reached the lower chambers, a figure blocked the passage.
Lydia Vance. Dressed in ceremonial robes. A cruel smile on her lips.
“I knew you’d come, cousin. You’re as predictable as your mother.”
Ella tensed. “I’m not your cousin.”
“She’s a traitor.” Lydia gestured, and a dozen armed guards emerged from side corridors. “Seize them.”
Ethan shifted—his body grew, claws extended. He tore through three guards before the others could react.
Ella used her vision to dodge a strike and grabbed a fallen guard’s dagger.
But Lydia was faster. She threw a blast of dark energy that sent Ella crashing into the wall.
“You think you can challenge the Council?” Lydia sneered. “You’re a half-breed whose only trick is seeing a few seconds ahead.”
Ella struggled to her feet. Her arm burned. The silver mark was glowing.
Then a voice echoed in her mind: *Daughter. Draw the moon’s power. Strike her with light.*
Ella raised her hand. She concentrated on the moon—the Blood Moon rising outside. A beam of silver light lanced from her palm, hitting Lydia’s chest.
Lydia stumbled back, robes smoking. “What—?”
Ethan finished off the last guard and grabbed Ella. “We don’t have much time. The ritual is starting.”
They ran down the stairs. The altar chamber was ahead.
Inside, the High Priest stood over a stone altar where Elena lay chained, pale and weak.
Marcus Vance stood at the head, a dark blade in his hand.
“Welcome, niece. You’re just in time for the final act.”
Ella charged.
But Marcus raised his hand, and a wave of force threw her back. She landed hard, her vision flickering.
Ethan lunged at Marcus, but the King’s guard intercepted—Jason, Ethan’s own second-in-command, holding a silver blade.
“I’m sorry, boss,” Jason said, face pained. “They have my family.”
Ethan gaped. “Jason?”
But Jason didn’t attack. He turned and stabbed the guard beside him.
“I’m not with them.” He threw Ethan a sword. “Go. I’ll hold the door.”
Ethan caught the sword and turned on Marcus.
The fight was brutal. Marcus was centuries old, fueled by stolen power. Ethan was younger, but his Lycan blood was pure.
Ella rose and ran to the altar. She pushed the chains, but they were enchanted.
“Mother.”
The name tore out of her—ten years of silence behind it.
Elena opened her eyes. Silver, like Ella’s. Thinner than any photograph. Hair dull against the stone.
“You came.” Elena’s voice broke on the second word. “My baby. You came.”
Ella gripped the bars until her knuckles whitened. “I’m here. I’m right here.”
“I watched you grow from far away. In flashes. In pain.” Elena pressed her fingers through the gap, searching for Ella’s skin. When they touched, both of them shuddered. “You’re so warm. I forgot what warm felt like.”
Ella leaned her forehead to the cold iron, as close as she could get. Tears ran hot down her cheeks. She didn’t try to stop them.
“I thought you were dead,” she said. “I thought I was alone.”
“Never alone.” Elena’s thumb traced her jaw, clumsy with weakness. “I sent what I could. The roses. The voice. Did you hear me?”
“I heard you.”
For a moment, the chamber faded—the chanting, Marcus, the Blood Moon overhead. There was only her mother’s hand on her face and the sound of two people breathing the same air again.
Then Elena’s eyes flicked toward the dais. Fear returned, sharp as a blade.
“You must flee. He is too strong.”
“No. I’m not leaving you.”
Elena’s hand tightened on Ella’s cheek. “The bond—you started it. You need to finish it. Only the full bond can give you the power to stop him.”
“With Ethan?”
“Yes. Now. While there is time.”
Ella looked back at Ethan fighting Marcus. He was bleeding. His arm was cut deep.
She reached through the bond. *Ethan. I’m ready. Do it now.*
He didn’t answer, but she felt his awareness. He threw a punch to create distance, then turned.
Marcus took the opening—a dagger of dark energy slammed into Ethan’s chest.
He dropped.
“Ethan!” Ella’s scream tore through the chamber.

## 第8章 The Bond

Ella threw herself at Marcus, a wave of silver fire erupting from her hands. He blocked, surprised by her strength. But she wasn’t aiming for him—she grabbed Ethan and pulled him behind the altar.
Ethan’s breath was shallow. Blood pooled under his chest.
“You’re going to be fine,” she said, tears streaming. “You have to be fine.”
He smiled weakly. “Finish it. The bond—our blood.”
Ella looked at her mother on the altar—Elena watching them, lips moving in a prayer Ella couldn’t hear. Then back at Ethan. The man who had waited. Who had not lied about the cost.
She cut her palm again. The pain was nothing.
“I promise,” she said. “I trust you.”
She pressed her blood to his. Their scars from the cabin flared, seeking each other.
“I—” The word stuck. Love was too large and too soon and exactly true.
She kissed him instead.
For a heartbeat, nothing happened. Then the mark flared. Power rushed through her—a door swinging open all the way.
She felt his life force draining, but she poured her own into him—her vision energy, her hybrid blood. The wound began to close.
Ethan’s eyes snapped open. The iris caught fire—gold and silver. He drew a shaky breath, human still, and caught her wrist.
“I’m here,” he said. Rough. Certain.
“I know.” She felt him through the bond—not just power, but presence. Steady.
Then he roared, shifting fully into a massive black wolf.
Marcus stepped back. “Impossible. The bond—it holds that much power?”
Ella stood. Silver flame wreathed her body. The bond hummed in her chest, Ethan’s heartbeat layered over hers. Power enough to save him. The realm would have to wait for the rest.
“It’s over, Uncle.”
Jason had cleared the remaining guards and was helping Elena off the altar.
Marcus laughed. “You think a bond makes you strong? Let me show you true power.”
He raised his hands. The altar chamber shook. The Blood Moon above bled into a black eclipse.
Elena screamed. “He’s activating the curse now—he’ll drain all hybrids!”
Ella looked at Ethan. He was huge, but he was hurting.
“Together,” she said.
He nodded.
They attacked as one. Ella’s silver flame and Ethan’s wolf strength. Marcus deflected them, but they kept pressing.
Ella saw his moves a second ahead. She called out directions to Ethan.
They forced Marcus back to the edge of the altar platform.
Then Marcus made a mistake. He overextended his shield.
Ella poured all her energy into a single spear of light. It pierced him through the chest.
Marcus stared, disbelieving. “You—you can’t—I am eternal—”
He crumpled.
The black eclipse shattered. The Blood Moon returned to normal.
And in the distance, the curse’s chains broke.
The chamber went still.

Ella fell to her knees. Elena caught her—real arms, thin but strong, pulling her close for the first time since Ella was a child. She smelled of stone and old blood and something underneath that was still, impossibly, her mother.
“I’ve got you,” Elena whispered into her hair. “I’ve got you now.”
Ella sobbed into her shoulder. All the years she had smiled in offices and counted to five and slept with the lights on poured out of her at once. She didn’t try to be brave. She clung to her mother and let herself shake.
“You did it, daughter.”
“You’re alive.” Ella pulled back enough to see her face. Traced the silver at her temples with trembling fingers. “You’re really alive.”
“For now.” Elena smiled through tears of her own. “For now is enough.”
Ethan hung back by the shattered altar, giving them space. Blood dried on his shirt. When Ella looked at him, he nodded once—not rushing her.
She wiped her face with the back of her hand. Her mother’s hand stayed in hers.
“We do it together,” Ella said. “All three of us.”
Elena squeezed her fingers. “Always.”
Ethan crossed the room then, slowly, and set his hand on Ella’s shoulder. She leaned into the touch without letting go of her mother.
Only when Elena’s breathing steadied did Jason speak from the doorway. “Boss. We need to move.”
Outside the chamber, horns sounded—loyalists rallying to retake the palace before dawn.

## 第9章 The Crown Trial

Marcus was dead, but the palace did not go quiet.

Hybrid prisoners broke their chains in the lower cells. Pureblood guards split—some fled, some knelt, some reached for weapons they no longer believed in. Ella stood in the throne room with Elena leaning against her shoulder and Ethan barking orders through Jason’s comm link.

A messenger burst in, breathless. “The northern packs won’t accept a hybrid on the throne. Bjorn of the Frost Pack is marching south with two thousand wolves. Three days, maybe less.”

Ethan swore. “We don’t have three days.”

Elena lifted her head. Weak, but clear. “Then use the old law. Any alpha who rejects the heir may challenge for the crown—single combat, wolf against wolf. Win, and the packs bound by blood must yield.”

Ella looked at her mother, then at the empty throne. Marcus’s body had already been dragged away. The crown itself sat on a side table like an accusation.

“Broadcast it,” she said. “I’ll fight Bjorn at dawn.”

Ethan caught her wrist. “You’re bleeding from three fights already.”

“So is he, if he’s marching all night.” She pulled free, gently. “If I don’t prove myself now, we’ll spend the next year at war. I won’t do that to them.”

The word went out before midnight.

---

Ella didn’t sleep.

Ethan found her on the battlements after midnight, arms wrapped around herself, silver mark hidden under a borrowed coat. Below, campfires dotted the road north—Bjorn’s vanguard, close enough to smell smoke.

“You should rest,” he said.

“I keep seeing his teeth at my throat.” She didn’t look at him. “What if the Dawn Wolf isn’t enough?”

“It was enough at the altar.”

“That was rage. This is a courtroom with teeth.” She finally turned. “If I lose, they don’t just take the crown. They take you. They take my mother. They take every hybrid who believed me for five minutes.”

Ethan stepped close and brushed a streak of dried blood from her cheek. “Then don’t lose.”

“Helpful.”

“I’m serious.” His voice dropped. “Show them what you showed me when you pulled me back from the cable. Not the prophecy. You.”

She held his gaze until her pulse steadied.

Dawn came gray and cold.

---

Bjorn arrived at first light—a scarred alpha who stood a head taller than Ethan in human form and twice as wide in wolf. Hundreds filled the courtyard: northern warriors, freed hybrids, palace staff who didn’t know yet which side would feed them.

“So you’re the half-blood queen,” Bjorn called up. “You don’t look like much.”

Ella stripped off her torn jacket. “Looks lie.”

They shifted.

Bjorn became a wall of brown fur and muscle. Ella’s form answered in silver and white, light streaking along her spine—the Dawn Wolf, raw and new and shaking with fatigue. A murmur rolled through the crowd.

They collided.

Bjorn was strong in the way of old alphas who had never lost territory. Every charge came heavy, meant to break ribs. Ella didn’t meet force with force. Her sight opened a heartbeat ahead—paw low, jaw high, feint left—and she moved where he would not be.

Minutes stretched. Her lungs burned. Bjorn slowed, surprised that she was still standing.

He lunged for her throat.

She saw it before he committed, dropped under the arc, and pinned him with her forepaw at his neck. Her muzzle pressed against his fur.

“Yield.”

For a long second, nothing moved.

Then Bjorn went still beneath her. His wolf lowered its head to the stones.

“I yield.”

The courtyard erupted.

Ella shifted back, naked and shaking, and accepted a cloak from Jason without looking away from Bjorn. She offered the alpha her hand. He took it, human again, blood on his lip.

“Rise, Alpha,” she said. “We fight the loyalists together now.”

Bjorn studied her face, then nodded once. “You have the Frost Pack.”

Elena was crying quietly in the front row. Ethan stood beside her, pride and fear warring in his eyes.

Ella crossed to her mother and knelt. “Did I do enough?”

“For today.” Elena cupped her face, thumbs brushing the tears Ella hadn’t noticed. “You were always enough. I just couldn’t tell you from where they kept me.”

Ella leaned into her mother’s hands and let herself breathe. The courtyard noise faded to a hum.

After a moment, Elena’s voice turned urgent again. “Tomorrow they’ll test you again. Before they do—take what your father left. Under the throne. The vault only opens for his bloodline, and you’re the last one standing.”

Ella looked at Ethan. “Now?”

“Now,” Elena said. “Before the next horn sounds.”

They ran back inside. Guards lifted the stone slab beneath the carpet. A spiral stair swallowed torchlight.

Ella took the first step down.

Two hundred feet below, something pulsed in the dark—and above them, distant as thunder, horns blared at the palace wall.

## 第10章 Father's Vault

The horns grew louder with every step.

Ella descended with a torch of silver fire in her palm. Ethan followed, blade drawn. The stair opened into a circular vault carved from black stone. At its center, a pedestal of obsidian held a sphere of liquid light.

Ancient words ringed the wall: *For the heir who completes the prophecy, the blood of the ancestor shall reveal the peace eternal.*

“Keep moving,” Ethan said. Dust shook loose from the ceiling. “Whatever’s up there isn’t waiting.”

Ella touched the sphere.

Images flooded her—the First King, the founding packs, the moon bargain, the feud between her father’s house and Ethan’s. She saw wolves kneeling not from fear but from law written in blood. Then the prophecy in silver script: *When she marks with the enemy line, the blood feud ends.*

She turned, breathless. “Our families were enemies for centuries. The bond wasn’t luck. It was the seal.”

Ethan’s face had gone pale. “Then the mark was always meant to be you.”

The sphere cracked. A glass-clear dagger floated into her grip—the Unity Blade, forged to bind packs by consent, not chains. It weighed almost nothing. It hummed against her palm like a living thing agreeing to be held.

The vault shuddered. Not magic—impact. A ram hitting the outer gate.

“We go,” Ethan said.

They climbed two hundred steps at a run and burst into the throne room. Jason met them, cheek split open, blood on his teeth.

“Five hundred loyalists at the wall,” he said. “Marcus’s old guard. They want the palace back before you can crown yourself queen of anything.”

Ella sheathed the Unity Blade at her hip. “Where’s Bjorn?”

“Holding the east wall with half his line. The other half broke when they saw the ram.”

Ella didn’t hesitate. She went out front.

The loyalist commander screamed from horseback. “She’s a half-breed witch! Take the palace back for pure blood!”

His wolves charged.

Ella stood in the gateway, silver fire crawling up her arms. “I am Aldric’s daughter. I carry the Unity Blade. Turn back, or answer to the moon.”

Some hesitated. Most did not.

The fight was ugly and short. Ella’s sight turned the lane into a trap—she called angles to Bjorn’s line as he rallied, cut down the ram crew with the blade’s flat, paralyzing without killing. Ethan tore through the center in half-shift, a blur of black fur and controlled rage. When the commander fell, the rest broke.

Silence settled over blood and stone.

Ella raised the blade so every survivor could see it. Torchlight ran along the glass edge like a second moon.

“Tomorrow at noon,” she said, voice carrying, “pureblood privilege dies in this hall. Hybrid and pure wolf stand equal under one law. Spread the word tonight.”

No one cheered. They were too tired. But they listened. Ella watched faces in the crowd—fear, calculation, the first fragile shape of belief.

Jason slumped against the wall, laughing once in disbelief. “That’s two wars in one day.”

Ella looked at the blade, then at Ethan’s bloodied knuckles, then at the sky where smoke still climbed.

“One day,” she said. “Two wins. We’re not done yet.”

She did not feel like a queen. She felt like a woman who had run out of stairs and found, at the bottom, the weapon her father had left and the man her mother’s prophecy had demanded. That would have to be enough until dawn.

Ethan wiped blood from his knuckles and stood beside her without touching the blade. “Tomorrow,” he said, “they sign or they leave.”

“Tomorrow,” she agreed, “we make them choose.”

Behind them, Bjorn’s wolves dragged the ram aside like discarded timber. The palace still smelled of smoke, but for the first time since Marcus fell, the wind off the wall carried no battle horn—only rain coming in from the north.

## 第11章 Blood and Law

The hall filled at noon.

Hybrids in borrowed clothes. Pureblood officers still wearing yesterday’s blood. Bjorn and his lieutenants on one side, Jason and the palace guard on the other. Elena sat in a chair at Ella’s left, wrapped in blankets, color slowly returning to her face.

Ella did not wear a crown yet. She didn’t need one to say what had to be said.

“The old blood laws are abolished,” she announced. “No wolf is property because of lineage. Hybrids get full standing in the Council. Trials by sniff without cause are banned. The dungeons where my mother was kept stay open as a monument—and then they burn.”

Murmurs. Anger. Relief. She let them wash over her. She had learned in corporate hallways how to stand still while people decided whether to hate you to your face.

Bjorn stepped forward first. “The Frost Pack accepts the charter.” He cut his palm and pressed it to the parchment Jason held.

One by one, others followed. Ink and blood mingled on the page. Ella watched each signature land like a nail driven into old wood.

Elena reached over and squeezed Ella’s hand when the room grew loud. “Your father wanted this,” she whispered. “Not the throne. The change.”

Ella swallowed hard and signed last. Her name looked too small for what it cost.

---

The attack came at sunset.

Not an army—a cell of Marcus’s old fanatics, six wolves in hoods, blades silver-tipped, aiming for Ethan as he escorted Ella back to the private wing. Ella’s sight flared: left blade, low; right cross; Ethan’s ribs exposed for half a second.

She shoved him sideways and took the slash across her forearm instead of his chest.

Pain white-hot. She spun, grabbed the attacker’s wrist, and drove her knee into his jaw. Ethan was already moving—two down, three running, one reaching for a second knife.

Ella threw the Unity Blade—not to kill, but to pin the last man’s cloak to the wall. He jerked, trapped. Guards poured in from both ends of the corridor.

When it was over, Ethan caught her uninjured hand, breathing hard. “You took a blade for me.”

“You would have done the same.” She leaned into him, dizzy.

Elena was waiting in Ella’s chambers, having refused to stay in bed. She cleaned the wound herself, old hands steady, humming a tune Ella half remembered from childhood.

“You’re going to be a stubborn queen,” she said.

“Learned from someone.”

Elena’s mouth twitched. Almost a smile. “Tomorrow, let them see you marked. Not as prophecy. As choice. The court needs to know you’re not alone on that throne.”

Ella looked at Ethan across the room. He met her eyes without flinching. Blood still dried dark on his collar. He had not asked her to perform courage today. He had simply stood where she stood.

“I want the whole kingdom to witness it,” she said.

Ethan crossed the room and knelt—not subject to queen, but man to woman. “Say when.”

“Tomorrow at moonrise.”

Outside, the city bells rang once, uneven, as if the palace itself was learning a new rhythm. Ella listened until the sound faded, then let her mother bandage the last inch of silver-burned skin and pretended, for one hour, that tomorrow could be only joy.

When Ethan finally left for his own rooms, he paused at the door. “Moonrise,” he said.

“I know.” She met his eyes. “I’ll be there.”

He nodded once and went. Ella sat with her mother’s hand in hers and watched the moon climb, slow and patient, as if it had been waiting for them all along.

The charter lay on the table beside them, ink dry, blood darkening at the edges. Ella read her own signature once more and watched the moon climb.

## 第12章 Marked Queen

Moonrise painted the courtyard silver.

Every pack that had signed the charter sent witnesses. Hybrids stood beside purebloods without segregation for the first time in living memory. Elena sat in the front row, wrapped in white fur, Jason and Bjorn flanking her like honor guard.

Ella wore a gown the color of ash with silver thread tracing the mark on her arm. Ethan wore black, his tie threaded with matching silver, jaw set against the stares that still cut.

The moon priest raised his hands. “Under the witness of the Blood Moon—”

A murmur ran through the crowd. Ella lifted her chin and let it pass. She had stood in worse rooms. She had survived worse nights.

“Do you, Ella Vance, take Ethan Blackwood as your true bonded, for life and beyond?”

The courtyard held its breath. Ella thought of the elevator. The cabin. The altar. Every moment she had almost walked away—and every moment she had stayed.

“I do.”

“Do you, Ethan Blackwood, take Ella Vance as your true bonded, for life and beyond?”

Ethan didn’t hesitate. His eyes never left hers. “I do.”

They cut their palms. Blood met blood.

Pain flared—then warmth. The scars from the altar, from the desperate night on the stone, flared bright and settled into a single pulsing line that ran wrist to wrist when their hands clasped.

The bond locked. What the altar had forged in blood, the moon now sealed by choice—not a contract, not a prophecy, but witnessed by every pack in the realm.

Ella felt it settle through her like a breath she had been holding for months.

The crowd howled.

She didn’t move at first. Neither did Ethan. They stood with their hands joined, forehead almost touching, letting the noise wash over them until it felt real.

Then she kissed him.

Ethan’s arms came around her, solid and real. She melted into it and let herself stay there a long moment before the world asked anything else of her.

When they parted, a strange flutter moved through her core—small, new, impossible to mistake.

Ethan felt it through the bond and went still. “Ella?”

She pressed his hand to her stomach, laughing once in disbelief. “Later.” She glanced at the crowd, at her mother in the front row. “Let them stare first.”

Elena stood, slowly, and the courtyard quieted. She didn’t speak long.

“She survived what I could not finish,” Elena said. “Watch her well.”

That was all. It was enough.

---

Later, on the balcony where Marcus once addressed loyalists, Ella leaned into Ethan’s shoulder. The city below was still scarred—broken gates, smoke stains, candles in windows—but alive.

“Are you afraid?” he asked.

“Of them?” She nodded toward the streets. “Every day. Of this?” She covered his hand on her belly. “No.”

Ethan was quiet a moment. “Your revenge is done.”

Ella watched the moon climb. “Then the rest is ours.”

Somewhere in the palace, Elena slept without chains for the first time in ten years. Somewhere in the city, a hybrid girl looked at her reflection and did not hide her eyes.

Ella laced her fingers through Ethan’s. The bond thrummed steady between them—not a chain, not a curse, but a choice made in public and kept in private.

Below them, the city lights flickered on one by one. Somewhere a hybrid child howled in celebration. Somewhere a pureblood officer threw his badge into the river. Change was never clean. It was only beginning.

Elena joined them on the balcony without announcement, wrapped in her white fur, and stood on Ella’s other side. No chains. No guards. Just a mother watching her daughter hold a kingdom and a mate at once.

“Ready?” he asked.

She smiled. “Not even close. But I’m not running.”
